Simulating Qubit Measurement Probabilities on a Classical Computer

Hypothesis: If a qubit is simulated on a classical computer with a set probability for 0 and 1, then after many measurements, the percentage of 0s and 1s will approximate the assigned probabilities, even though each measurement is random.

Step 1 - Set the Rotation Angle

In a real quantum computer, you don't set a probability directly.
You rotate the qubit by an angle θ and use the Born Rule: P(0) = cos²(θ/2)

How Does This Work?

A qubit is like a special coin. Instead of heads or tails, it gives 0 or 1.
But unlike a normal coin, you can make it biased — more likely to land on one side.

In a real quantum computer, this is done by rotating the qubit by an angle θ on something called the Bloch Sphere.
The probability is then calculated automatically using the Born Rule:

P(0) = cos²(θ/2)

This simulator does the same math to replicate how a real qubit works!

Note on the Born Rule:
While running this experiment, I wondered how a real quantum computer actually sets a qubit's probability. After researching, I found that you physically rotate the qubit by an angle θ on the Bloch Sphere and the probability is calculated automatically using the Born Rule: P(0) = cos²(θ/2). I updated my simulator to do the same, making it more accurate to real quantum mechanics.
