Hypothesis

If a qubit is simulated on a classical computer with a set probability for 0 and 1, then after many measurements, the percentage of 0s and 1s will approximate the assigned probabilities — even though each individual measurement is random.

🤔 What is this experiment about?

The big idea

Quantum computers are weird

Normal computers use bits — they're always exactly 0 or 1. Quantum computers use qubits, which can be both at once until you look at them.

The question

Can we fake it?

Can a regular computer pretend to be a qubit? This simulator does exactly that — using random numbers to mimic quantum measurement.

What we test

Does the pattern hold?

One result is random. But after 100 tries, does the total match the probability we set? That's the experiment!

🪙

Think of it like a weighted coin flip

A normal coin is 50/50 heads or tails. But imagine a loaded coin — maybe it lands heads 70% of the time. You can't predict any single flip, but flip it 100 times and you'll get roughly 70 heads and 30 tails. That's exactly how a qubit works — and that's what this simulator tests!

📖 Glossary — words you might not know
Qubit
Like a computer's bit, but quantum. It can be 0, 1, or both at once until measured.
Superposition
A qubit being "both 0 and 1 at the same time" before you look at it. Measuring forces it to pick one.
Probability
How likely something is to happen. 70% chance of 0 means: on average, 70 out of 100 flips will be 0.
Independent variable (IV)
What we change on purpose. Here: the probability setting (50/50, 70/30, etc.)
Dependent variable (DV)
What we measure. Here: how many 0s and 1s we actually get out of 100 flips.
Deviation
How far off the actual result is from what we expected. Small deviation = simulator is working well!
